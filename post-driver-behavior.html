Driver Behavior Analysis — Exploratory Data Analysis

February 2026

I chose this topic partly because of my interest in automotive subjects. I am currently working on another project focused on vehicle information from car sales websites, so a driver-focused analysis felt like a natural complement — shifting attention from the car itself to the person behind the wheel.

The Research Question

The central question driving this analysis was: what sensor signals most clearly distinguish aggressive, distracted, and normal driving behavior? More specifically — do aggressive drivers consistently show higher speed and harder braking? Is phone usage a reliable indicator of distraction, or do signals like lane deviation and reaction time tell a richer story? And which features best separate the three behavior categories?

The Dataset

The dataset was sourced from Kaggle and simulates onboard diagnostic (OBD) and accelerometer readings captured during individual driving events. Each of the 30,000 rows represents a single moment in time, described by 11 sensor-based features: speed, forward and lateral acceleration, brake pressure, steering angle, throttle position, lane deviation, phone usage, headway distance (gap to the car ahead), reaction time, and a behavior label — either Aggressive, Distracted, or Normal. There were no missing values, and all three classes were exactly balanced at 10,000 observations each — a clear sign of synthetic generation, but also a convenient property for fair group comparisons.

What the Data Showed

The box plots were the most revealing part of the analysis. Brake pressure turned out to be the single strongest separator between groups: aggressive drivers apply dramatically harder braking, while normal drivers brake most gently. Speed followed a similar pattern — aggressive drivers run faster, though distracted and normal drivers are much closer to each other, meaning speed alone cannot tell those two groups apart.

Lane deviation was the defining signature of distracted driving. Distracted drivers drifted the most from lane center, consistent with divided attention. Aggressive drivers also showed above-normal deviation, but for a different reason — likely high-speed maneuvering rather than inattention. Headway distance told a complementary story: aggressive drivers maintained the shortest following gap (tailgating), while normal drivers kept the largest.

Phone usage was almost entirely concentrated in the distracted group, with near-zero rates in the other two. But it is not sufficient on its own — the scatter plots and box plots showed meaningful variation in lane deviation and reaction time within the distracted group regardless of phone use. The core signature of distracted driving in this dataset is really a combination of phone usage, elevated lane deviation, and a wider, more erratic reaction time distribution.

One finding that stood out: aggressive drivers had fast reaction times — not slow ones. This might seem counterintuitive, but it reflects the nature of high-risk driving. These drivers are constantly reacting to the high-intensity situations they create, not being cautious. Distracted drivers, by contrast, scattered across a wide reaction time range, consistent with inconsistent attention.

The correlation matrix confirmed that most features are weakly correlated with each other, meaning each visualization contributed independent information. The one exception was a moderate positive correlation between speed and throttle — expected, since going faster requires more throttle. Everything else was largely independent.

Limitations

The main caveat here is that the data is synthetic. Real telematics data would include road type, speed limits, weather, and time of day — context that fundamentally changes the meaning of a reading. 100 km/h on a highway is normal; the same speed on a residential street is dangerous. Without that context, patterns in this dataset partly reflect the assumptions built into how the data was generated rather than actual human behavior. Additionally, observations are treated as independent, when in reality readings from a single driver form a correlated time series.

Takeaway

No single sensor reading reliably identifies a driver's behavior — group overlap in the scatter plots makes that clear. But the combination of speed, brake pressure, lane deviation, and reaction time produces coherent and distinct profiles for each group. The analysis confirmed that distraction and aggression are behaviorally distinct: aggression is characterized by intensity and speed, while distraction shows up as inattention and drift — two fundamentally different failure modes that would require different detection strategies in a real safety system.
