Ethically Ambiguous Data Modeling

January 2026

Data modeling can be defined as a technical activity where data is collected and the best model to use for the data is selected and tested for performance. However, it should be noted that most data models operate in ethically ambiguous spaces. Ethically ambiguous data modeling can be defined as the creation and use of models whose social impacts are uncertain, unevenly distributed, or misaligned with the intent suggested by the models' technical success.

The question of what makes a data modeling practice ethically ambiguous is complex. It does not necessarily imply wrongdoing or the failure to adhere to best practices. Data models often make use of incomplete and biased data sets that are representative of past inequalities rather than the truth. Other data models may be based on proxy variables that indirectly discriminate against certain groups of people. Data models may also be technically successful but fail to address the social inequalities that exist among the people.

To responsibly operate in these gray areas, several foundational practices are necessary. First, data auditing is critical in recognizing where there is a lack of data, biased representations, or poor proxies. This allows the modeler to make better decisions rather than assuming the data is neutral. Second, stakeholder engagement is critical in grounding the model in the lived experience. Interacting with the communities or stakeholders that the model will impact can provide additional context to the modeler on the risks that the model is taking on, as well as whether the model’s purpose is actually in alignment with the values of the community.

For example, in the creation of a hiring screening model that accurately predicts employee performance, the model is technically successful. However, the model might actually perpetuate the same hiring biases that the data set was originally built on, inadvertently excluding qualified employees from underrepresented communities. The gray area here is not in the modeler’s intent, but in the model’s impact.

Ultimately, the key to successful data modeling is less about finding the right answer and more about understanding the ambiguity in the process.
